When Travel Plans Change Suddenly — Know Your Rights First

Last year airlines worldwide paid out over $7 billion in passenger compensation. But millions of passengers who were entitled to claim never filed — because they didn't know they had the right. The information below takes 5 minutes to read and can save you hundreds of euros.

US DOT 24-hour rule

For tickets booked with US airlines, you can cancel within 24 hours of booking at no charge — even Basic Economy. This applies when booking at least 7 days before departure. Not all non-US airlines offer this voluntarily, though many do.

Real incident

The traveller who didn't read the fare conditions

A business traveller booked the cheapest available fare — Basic Economy — on a Bangkok–London route, saving about £80 compared to Flex. Two weeks before departure, a meeting was rescheduled and the travel date needed to change.

Basic Economy: not changeable, not refundable. The traveller had to buy a completely new ticket at last-minute prices.

Net loss: £800 for a new last-minute ticket, minus the £80 saved. The Flex ticket would have cost £80 more and allowed a free change. The saving became the most expensive decision of the trip.

EU261: The Real Compensation Figures

EU261 applies to flights (1) departing from any EU airport, or (2) arriving in the EU operated by an EU-based airline. This covers most flights to and through Europe.

≤ 1,500 km€250€250
1,500–3,500 km€400€400
> 3,500 km€600€600

Beyond compensation, the airline must also provide:

  • Meals and refreshments during the wait (if delayed over 2 hours)
  • Hotel accommodation and transfers (if overnight stay required)
  • 2 phone calls, emails or faxes
  • Choice of: rebooked flight or full refund
⚠️
The extraordinary circumstances exception

Airlines don't have to pay compensation when the delay or cancellation is caused by extraordinary circumstances — severe weather, air traffic control strikes, political unrest, or security threats. They must still provide care (meals, hotel), but the cash compensation can be waived. Airlines sometimes overuse this exception — if you believe it doesn't apply, push back.

How to File an EU261 Claim Successfully

  1. Collect evidence immediately — photograph the departure board showing the actual time, keep every boarding pass, and save receipts for any food, hotel or transport you paid for during the delay.

  2. Submit to the airline first — use their official complaint form. You have up to 6 years to claim for EU flights (varies by country). Be specific: state the regulation (EU261/2004), the delay duration, and the compensation amount you're entitled to.

  3. If rejected: escalate to the national authority — each EU country has a National Enforcement Body (NEB). In Germany: Luftfahrt-Bundesamt. In France: DGAC. In the UK (post-Brexit): Civil Aviation Authority.

🔐
Insider Secret: claim services charge 25–35% commission

Services like AirHelp or ClaimCompass handle EU261 claims for a 25–35% cut of your compensation. For a €600 claim, that's €150–210 in fees. It's worth filing yourself first — the process is straightforward. Only use a service if the airline rejects your direct claim.

Real incident — successful claim

Bangkok–Frankfurt delayed 4 hours: €600 recovered

A passenger's Lufthansa flight from Bangkok to Frankfurt was delayed 4 hours 15 minutes due to a technical fault on the previous rotation. On arrival, the passenger filed a claim directly via the Lufthansa website citing EU261/2004, the 4+ hour delay, and the distance of over 3,500 km.

€600 compensation received 3 weeks later, directly into the bank account. No service fee required. The technical fault did not qualify as an extraordinary circumstance — it was within the airline's control.

Pre-Trip Checklist: Protecting Your Booking

✈️ Checklist: Flight Changes & Rights
Do these before and during every trip — prevents most financial losses from disruptions
Read fare conditions before buyingSpecifically: is it changeable? Is it refundable? What's the fee? Don't assume.
Consider Flex if your dates might changeThe extra €50–80 for a Flex fare often costs less than a single change fee.
Screenshot your booking confirmationAirlines sometimes change prices or conditions — your confirmation locks in what you agreed to.
At the airport: photograph the departure board immediately if delayedThis is your primary evidence for the EU261 claim. Timestamp matters.
Keep all boarding passes — every leg, including unused onesYou'll need them for any compensation claim. Digital or physical both work.
Save food and hotel receipts during delaysYou can claim these back under EU261's care obligations — keep originals or photos.
File EU261 claim yourself before using a serviceDirect claims cost nothing. Services take 25–35%. Try direct first — it's usually straightforward.

The bottom line

You have more rights than airlines communicate. EU261 is real, legally binding, and millions of euros go unclaimed every year simply because passengers don't know to ask. The 5 minutes spent understanding your rights are worth hundreds of euros when disruption strikes.
