The Real Numbers Behind Passenger Rights

Lost baggage SDR limits, EU261 by distance, which framework applies when — with step-by-step claims

When an Airline Causes Loss — Two Frameworks Protect You

Most passengers don't know that airline liability has a defined ceiling — or that those ceilings are much larger than most people expect. Even more critically, most don't know the filing deadlines. Miss them, and you lose all rights regardless of how legitimate the claim is.

Montreal Convention 1999: Real Liability Numbers

The Montreal Convention is an international treaty establishing airline liability limits for baggage and passenger injury. Amounts are denominated in SDR (Special Drawing Rights), an IMF unit of account that fluctuates daily against major currencies.

⚠️
Ceiling, not automatic payment

1,288 SDR is the maximum the airline must pay — you must provide evidence of actual value lost. For high-value items (electronics, jewellery, professional equipment), consider a Special Declaration of Interest at check-in, which raises the liability ceiling for a fee. Travel insurance usually covers these gaps more cost-effectively.

Lost Baggage: What to Do Before Leaving the Airport

The most common and costly mistake passengers make is leaving the airport without filing a formal report. Once you exit, your rights change significantly.

  1. Before leaving baggage claim — go to the airline's Lost & Found desk and file a Property Irregularity Report (PIR). Get the reference number in writing before leaving.

  2. Photograph everything immediately — your baggage claim tags, the PIR form, and the empty carousel. Timestamped photos are primary evidence.

  3. Keep every receipt for emergency purchases — clothing, toiletries and essentials bought because your bag didn't arrive are reimbursable. No receipt = no reimbursement.

  4. File a formal written claim with the airline within the deadline — 7 days for damage, 21 days for delayed bags. Missing these deadlines extinguishes all rights under the Convention.

  5. Track via the PIR reference number — if the bag is confirmed lost (typically after 21 days), initiate formal compensation in writing citing the Montreal Convention.

🚨
Hard deadlines — no exceptions
  • Damaged baggage: PIR within 7 days of receiving the bag
  • Delayed baggage: Written claim within 21 days of receiving it
  • Lost baggage: Formal claim within 2 years of scheduled arrival
  • Missing any deadline completely voids your right to claim under the Convention
Real incident — claim won

Bag lost on Bangkok–Frankfurt connection: €1,200 recovered

A passenger's checked bag didn't arrive at Frankfurt after a BKK–DXB–FRA routing. Before exiting baggage claim, they filed a PIR and got a reference number. Over the following days they bought essential clothing and toiletries, saving every receipt. The bag was confirmed lost after 23 days. A formal written claim citing the Montreal Convention was submitted to the airline.

Total recovered: €840 for bag contents (proven via receipts and card statements) plus €360 in emergency purchase reimbursements. The PIR filed before leaving the airport was the critical first step — without it the airline could have denied the claim entirely.

💡
EU261 scope reminder

EU261 applies to: (1) flights departing any EU airport regardless of airline nationality, or (2) flights arriving in the EU operated by an EU-registered carrier. A non-EU airline flying Bangkok to Frankfurt does not trigger EU261 for the Bangkok leg — but it would for the Frankfurt arrival if operated by a European carrier.

Step-by-Step Claims

EU261: delay, cancellation, denied boarding

  1. Collect evidence at the airport — photograph the departures board showing the delay, keep all boarding passes, save receipts for meals or hotel during the wait.

  2. Submit directly to the airline first — use their official complaints form. Reference "Regulation EC 261/2004" and state the specific compensation amount you're entitled to. Airlines have up to 6 weeks to respond.

  3. If rejected: escalate to the National Enforcement Body (NEB) — every EU country has one. Germany: Luftfahrt-Bundesamt. France: DGAC. Austria: Schienen-Control GmbH. NEBs investigate free of charge.

Montreal Convention: baggage claims

  1. File PIR at the airport immediately — mandatory first step. Keep the reference number.

  2. Submit formal written claim within the deadline — include the PIR reference, value evidence, and emergency purchase receipts.

  3. If undervalued or rejected — escalate through national consumer protection bodies or pursue via civil courts. Travel insurance often covers gaps between airline compensation and actual loss.

🔐
The Special Declaration of Interest — use it for valuable checked items

At check-in, you can declare a higher value for luggage containing expensive items, raising the airline's liability ceiling above 1,288 SDR for an additional fee. For laptops, professional gear or jewellery, this is worth considering — though dedicated travel insurance typically covers these more cost-effectively.

The bottom line

Both frameworks are legally binding and regularly produce real compensation. The difference between getting paid and getting nothing usually comes down to filing the PIR before leaving the airport, and submitting a written claim within the deadline. The paperwork takes 20 minutes and can be worth hundreds to thousands of euros.
