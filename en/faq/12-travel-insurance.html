Travel Insurance 2026
What You Actually Get When You Pay

What's covered, what's not, the most common reasons claims are rejected, and 5 questions to ask before buying any policy

Why Claims Get Rejected: The Patterns

Every year thousands of travellers file travel insurance claims and receive partial payment or a full rejection. The cause is almost never a policy problem — it's almost always something in the exclusions that wasn't read before purchase. The most important section of any travel insurance policy is the list of what isn't covered.

What Standard Policies Usually Don't Cover

This is the section most travellers skip — and the section that explains most rejected claims.

🚨
Pre-existing medical conditions

If you have an existing condition and it flares up while travelling, standard policies almost always exclude it. This includes managed conditions like diabetes, asthma, or heart conditions. To be covered, you need to either declare the condition and pay a higher premium, or purchase a specialist policy that explicitly includes it.

⚠️
Travelling against a government travel warning

If your government has issued a "do not travel" or "travel warning" advisory for your destination and you travel anyway, most policies will not pay out for any claim connected to the warned situation. Always check your government's travel advisories before booking.

  • Adventure sports — bungee jumping, skydiving, motorbiking (some), scuba diving (some) typically require a separate rider or specialist policy
  • "Cancel for Any Reason" (CFAR) — if you simply don't want to travel anymore, standard cancellation doesn't apply; CFAR must be purchased as an add-on, usually within 10–21 days of booking, and typically only reimburses 50–75% of costs
  • High-value items not declared — expensive cameras, jewellery, laptops over a certain value must be listed on the policy before the trip; undeclared items are typically not covered beyond a low standard limit
  • Losses without documentation — theft claims without a police report, baggage claims without a PIR, medical claims without receipts are all routinely rejected
Real claim — preventable rejection

Medical claim rejected in the USA — pre-existing condition not declared

A traveller with well-managed high blood pressure had a cardiac event while in New York. The US hospital bill came to $47,000. They filed a claim with their standard travel insurance expecting full coverage.

Claim rejected. The policy excluded pre-existing cardiovascular conditions. The insurer argued the cardiac event was connected to the undisclosed condition. The traveller paid $47,000 out of pocket. A specialist policy declaring the condition would have cost approximately €80 more. The $47,000 difference was entirely preventable.

5 Things to Check Before Buying

  1. Medical coverage limit — Japan and the USA have extremely high healthcare costs. Minimum €3 million coverage is recommended for both. Many budget policies only cover €1 million, which can be insufficient for a serious situation in the US.

  2. Cashless or reimbursement — Cashless treatment means the insurer pays the hospital directly; you don't pay out of pocket. Reimbursement means you pay first and claim back later. Cashless is significantly better, especially for large amounts.

  3. 24-hour emergency hotline accessible from abroad — Check that the number works internationally and that there's multilingual support. Test it before you need it.

  4. Pre-existing condition coverage — If you have any managed health condition, check explicitly whether it's covered. "Pre-existing" in policy language is often broader than you'd expect.

  5. Adventure sports clause — If your trip involves diving, skiing, motorbikes, or any activity beyond standard sightseeing, verify it's included or add a rider.

Credit Card Travel Insurance: Useful Backup, Unreliable Primary

Platinum and above credit cards often include automatic travel insurance when the ticket is paid with that card. This sounds comprehensive but has significant limitations:

💡
What credit card travel insurance usually provides
  • Emergency medical: typically €1–2 million (often insufficient for USA)
  • Trip delay and missed connection compensation
  • Some baggage coverage
⚠️
What credit card insurance usually doesn't provide
  • Trip cancellation (the single most valuable benefit of dedicated policies)
  • Pre-existing condition coverage
  • Adventure sports coverage
  • Full ticket must be paid on that single card — split payments typically invalidate the cover
  • Coverage if your card has an annual fee you've waived or changed to a lower tier
🔐
The documentation checklist most travellers build too late

Every successful claim rests on documentation. Start collecting before the trip: keep all booking confirmation emails. During any incident: file a police report for any theft (required by almost all policies), get a PIR at the airport for any baggage issue before leaving, keep every receipt related to the incident, and get the medical diagnosis in writing. Without documentation, even a valid claim often fails.

Travel Insurance Checklist

What to verify before purchase and what to prepare before departure
Check medical limit for your destinationUSA and Japan: minimum €3 million. Europe: €1 million is usually adequate. Budget policies often fall short for high-cost destinations.
Declare pre-existing conditions before purchasingNon-declaration is the single biggest cause of rejected medical claims. Paying extra for declaration is far cheaper than a rejected claim abroad.
Verify adventure sports are covered if relevantScuba diving, skiing, motorbikes — check the sports exclusion list explicitly before buying.
Keep all booking confirmations and receiptsEvery claim requires proof of purchase, proof of the incident, and proof of the loss. Start collecting from day one.
File a police report for any theft immediatelyRequired by almost all policies. Filing 3 days later is often too late and gives the insurer grounds to reject.

✅
The bottom line

Travel insurance works well for the people who read what it covers before they need it — and fails the people who assume it covers everything. The policies are not the problem; the assumptions are. Five minutes of reading exclusions before purchase is the difference between a solved problem and a €47,000 bill.
