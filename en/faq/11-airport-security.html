CT Scanners, New Rules & How to Clear Faster

What changed in 2026, which airports no longer require removing laptops, what gets flagged most, and how to clear 10–15 minutes faster

What Changed at Security in 2026

Frequent travellers who prepared the same way in 2019 and 2026 found very different experiences. CT scanner technology has rolled out to major hubs, changing the rules in those lanes while legacy X-ray lanes at the same airports remain unchanged. Understanding which technology your lane uses is now the most important piece of pre-security preparation.

Airports with CT scanners installed (2026 status)

  • London Heathrow LHR — T2 and T3 complete, liquid restriction lifted in CT lanes
  • Amsterdam AMS — Main departure halls, ongoing expansion
  • Dubai DXB — Terminal 3, some Terminal 1 lanes
  • Singapore Changi T4 — Full CT rollout complete
  • Bangkok Suvarnabhumi BKK — Partial rollout, expanding 2026
  • Most US major airports — TSA PreCheck lanes use CT, standard lanes mixed
⚠️
Never assume which technology your lane uses

The same terminal may have CT lanes and X-ray lanes side by side. Look for signage or ask the officer before placing your bag. Assuming CT rules in an X-ray lane means your bag is going through secondary screening.

How to Prepare for Maximum Speed

The passengers who clear security fastest don't move faster at the belt — they prepare in the queue. Everything below can be done while waiting, before you reach the conveyor.

  1. Remove laptop and tablet (unless lane signage says CT — confirmed, not assumed). Place in a separate bin, flat.

  2. Remove your liquids bag and place on top of your main bag or separately in a bin.

  3. Remove belt, watch, coins, keys — put them inside your bag or a jacket pocket before reaching the belt. Don't fill a bin with small items.

  4. Shoes off if required — mandatory in the US and some other countries, not standard in EU. Check the lane signage.

  5. Put your bag on the belt last — send loose items and laptop bin first, then your bag. This keeps the sequence correct when collecting on the other side.

Real situation — avoidable secondary screening

Frankfurt — flagged for tangled cables and forgotten power bank

A business traveller placed their laptop in a bin but left a power bank inside a bag pocket and had multiple charging cables bundled together in the main compartment. The bag was flagged, pulled aside, and opened for manual inspection.

12 minutes of additional screening. Both issues were preventable: the power bank should have been placed in a bin (or at minimum the top of the bag), and cables laid flat or coiled neatly. Dense tangles of cable look identical to concealed items on a 2D X-ray scan.
On the return journey the same traveller placed the power bank in a separate bin and untangled cables in a small pouch — cleared in under 90 seconds with no secondary screening.

🔐
Duty-free alcohol at connecting airports

If you buy duty-free alcohol at your origin airport and have a connection, you may face secondary security at the connecting airport. The alcohol is only safe if it's in a sealed STEB (Security Tamper-Evident Bag) with the original receipt showing it was purchased within the past 24 hours. Do not open the STEB bag until you reach your final destination.

The bottom line

Security preparation is a skill that compounds. Travellers who spend 3 minutes preparing in the queue clear security faster, get flagged less, and catch flights with less stress. The rules have changed — the preparation approach doesn't need to be complicated, just updated.
