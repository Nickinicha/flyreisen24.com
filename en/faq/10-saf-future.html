SAF & Sustainable Aviation
2026–2035: What's Actually Changing

Sustainable Aviation Fuel, electric aircraft timelines, EU mandates and what it means for ticket prices

Why Aviation Is Harder to Decarbonise Than Everything Else

Aviation accounts for roughly 2–3% of global CO₂ emissions — but when non-CO₂ effects are included (contrail formation, NOx emissions at altitude), the actual climate impact may be 3–4 times higher. The challenge is that unlike road transport, the electrification solution that works for cars doesn't scale to long-haul flights.

The core problem is energy density. The battery required to power a wide-body aircraft across the Pacific would weigh several times more than the aircraft itself. That physical constraint means long-haul aviation will need a liquid fuel for decades — and SAF is the most viable option currently available.

What SAF Is — and What It Isn't

Sustainable Aviation Fuel is aviation fuel produced from non-fossil sources, engineered to be a drop-in replacement for conventional Jet-A. "Drop-in" means it can be blended with existing fuel and used in current aircraft engines without modification — a critical advantage over alternatives like hydrogen, which would require entirely new aircraft and airport infrastructure.

🛢️ Conventional Jet-A

  • Refined from fossil crude oil
  • Releases CO₂ stored underground for millions of years
  • Significantly cheaper than SAF (currently)
  • Mature global supply chain
  • Open carbon cycle — net new CO₂

🌿 SAF

  • Produced from waste, biomass or captured CO₂
  • Reduces lifecycle CO₂ by 50–85%
  • 2–5× more expensive than Jet-A (currently)
  • Supply still ~0.1% of market
  • Closed carbon cycle — CO₂ released was previously absorbed

What SAF can be made from

The range of feedstocks is broader than most people realise, which is a key advantage — no single source dependency:

  • Agricultural and forestry residues — straw, wood chips, rice husks, harvest waste — doesn't compete with food production
  • Municipal solid waste and used cooking oil — non-recyclable waste streams and food industry fat waste
  • Power-to-Liquid (e-SAF) — captures CO₂ from air, combines with renewable electricity to synthesise fuel — near-zero lifecycle emissions
  • Algae and energy crops — high oil yield per hectare, can grow on non-agricultural land

What this means for passengers departing EU airports

Any flight departing Paris, Frankfurt, Amsterdam or any other EU airport is subject to these mandates — regardless of which airline operates it or where the flight is going. The fuel blend will increasingly include SAF over the next decade, with costs gradually passing through to ticket prices.

Impact on Passengers: Prices and New Options

SAF Contribution Programs

Several airlines already offer voluntary SAF contribution options at booking — Lufthansa, British Airways and Singapore Airlines among them. Passengers pay an additional €5–30 per flight to fund SAF purchases for a proportional share of that flight's fuel.

⚠️
SAF vs carbon offsets — know the difference

SAF physically reduces CO₂ in the fuel lifecycle and is measured under standards like CORSIA. Carbon offsets purchase emission credits from third-party projects (forests, wind farms) — quality varies enormously and several major offset schemes have faced credibility challenges. Airlines that buy actual SAF typically report verified quantities in tonnes in their sustainability reports. If an airline advertises "eco-friendly" or "carbon neutral" without disclosing actual SAF volumes, that warrants scrutiny.

Ticket price impact

At current blending ratios of 2–6%, the direct cost impact on ticket prices is minimal. In the medium term, as mandates rise toward 20%+ by 2035, a 5–15% price increase on long-haul flights is plausible according to IATA estimates. However, SAF production costs are falling rapidly as scale increases — e-SAF could approach conventional Jet-A prices by 2035–2040.

Real example — what SAF flight experience is like

Singapore Airlines SAF flight: completely normal for passengers

In late 2023, Singapore Airlines conducted a commercial flight using 100% SAF on the Singapore–London route. The result: engines operated normally in every way, passengers noticed no difference whatsoever in their on-board experience, and CO₂ was reduced by approximately 70–80% compared to a standard flight. SAF doesn't change the passenger experience — it changes what happens in the supply chain before the fuel enters the tank.

The bottom line

Aviation decarbonisation is real, mandatory (at least in the EU), and accelerating. SAF is not a distant concept — it's already blended into the fuel of flights departing European airports. The passenger experience changes nothing. What changes is the supply chain and, gradually, the price. Understanding the timeline helps set realistic expectations and spot genuine sustainability claims from greenwashing.
